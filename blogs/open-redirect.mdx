Most bug hunters eventually run into Open Redirects. If you spend enough time reading bounty discussions, you will see the same comments:

> "Open Redirects are low severity."
>
> "They do not deserve a bounty."
>
> "They are just redirects."

For a long time, I agreed.

<div style={styles.lead}>
      <strong>Core idea:</strong> the redirect itself is usually not the real impact. Trust is.
      An attacker can borrow the reputation of a legitimate domain and weaponize that trust.
</div>

When combined with authentication logic, third-party services, or social engineering, a "simple" redirect can become much more interesting.

## A Simple Scenario

Imagine you receive these links in an email:

<div style={styles.grid}>
      <MiniCard title="Link A">
            <code>https://totally-legit-login.com</code>
      </MiniCard>
      <MiniCard title="Link B">
            <code>https://trusted-company.com/redirect?next=https://totally-legit-login.com</code>
      </MiniCard>
</div>

Which one feels more convincing at first glance?

Most users trust Link B because the visible domain appears familiar. Attackers rely on that split-second decision and on the fact that people rarely inspect every query parameter.

## What Happens Behind the Scenes

Redirects are normal in web applications. After login, users often move from one path to another:

```text
/login -> /dashboard
```

One common implementation looks like this:

```http
https://example.com/login?next=/dashboard
```

The issue starts when `next` is fully user-controlled without strict validation:

```http
https://example.com/login?next=https://attacker.com
```

A typical server response:

```http
HTTP/1.1 302 Found
Location: https://attacker.com
```

The browser follows `Location` immediately. No exploit chain is required at this step. It is just misplaced trust.

## Why This Bug Appears So Often

Open Redirects usually come from convenience, not malicious design.

- Returning users to their original page after auth
- Payment handoff and callback flows
- OAuth or SSO integrations
- Marketing and campaign redirects
- Third-party workflow transitions

The pattern is simple: UX improves, validation is skipped, trust boundary is crossed.

## Common Beginner Mistake

Many testers try one payload and stop:

```text
https://attacker.com
```

URL parsing is often flexible enough that alternate payload formats behave differently:

```text
//attacker.com
@attacker.com
.attacker.com
%2f%2fattacker.com
```

Blocking one shape does not mean the redirect is truly safe.

## The Real Value: Chaining

The most interesting reports are rarely standalone redirects. They are chain enablers.

<div style={styles.flow}>
Trusted Website
      -> Authentication Flow
      -> Open Redirect
      -> Attacker Infrastructure
</div>

<div style={styles.flow}>
Trusted Website
      -> Third-Party Service
      -> JavaScript Redirect
      -> Malicious Website
</div>

Open Redirect is the bridge, not the destination.

## How I Hunt for Open Redirects

I start with redirect-like parameters:

```text
redirect=
redirect_to=
next=
return=
return_to=
continue=
callback=
goto=
destination=
url=
r=
u=
```

Then I pivot into high-signal features:

- OAuth flows
- SSO flows
- Login and logout handlers
- Payment redirects
- Password reset journeys
- Third-party handoff pages

Finally, I ask chain-focused questions:

```text
Can I fully control destination?
Can I partially control destination?
Can I inject special characters?
Can I chain this with another feature?
Can I bypass warnings or allowlists?
```

Those questions are often more valuable than the base bug itself.

## Mindset Shift

The biggest lesson from Open Redirects is not about redirects. It is about thinking in systems.

Beginners often ask:

> Is this vulnerable?

Experienced researchers usually ask:

> What can this become?

Alone, Open Redirects may be low impact. Combined with phishing, auth workflows, third-party trust, or another bug, they can materially raise risk.

Security is rarely about isolated findings. It is about how pieces connect.
